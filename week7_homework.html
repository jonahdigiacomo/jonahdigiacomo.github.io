1 Dataset & Research Question

I used 2023 MLB Team Statistics (Baseball Reference) covering all 30 teams across the full 162-game season. The dataset includes offensive output (runs scored, home runs, batting average) and pitching performance (ERA), with teams grouped by division.

Research question: How does home run production relate to total runs scored, and which teams stand out at the extremes?

4 Chart 2: Top 10 Teams by Runs Scored

A labeled horizontal bar chart. No legend is used because values are annotated on each bar.

Code 
from pybaseball import team_batting
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

# Pull 2023 team batting stats
df = team_batting(2023)
df = df.rename(columns={"R": "Runs", "Team": "Team"})

RUNS_PER_HR = 1.4

top10 = df.nlargest(10, "Runs").sort_values("Runs").copy()
top10["HR_Runs"]    = (top10["HR"] * RUNS_PER_HR).round().astype(int)
top10["Other_Runs"] = top10["Runs"] - top10["HR_Runs"]

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(10, 6))

hr_colors    = ["#c0392b" if t == "Atlanta Braves" else "#e07b6a" for t in top10["Team"]]
other_colors = ["#922b21" if t == "Atlanta Braves" else "#2e5fa3" for t in top10["Team"]]

bars_other = ax.barh(top10["Team"], top10["Other_Runs"],
                     color=other_colors, edgecolor="white", height=0.65, label="Other Runs")
bars_hr    = ax.barh(top10["Team"], top10["HR_Runs"],
                     left=top10["Other_Runs"],
                     color=hr_colors, edgecolor="white", height=0.65, label="Runs via HR")

# Total label at end of each bar
for bar_o, (_, row) in zip(bars_other, top10.iterrows()):
    y_center = bar_o.get_y() + bar_o.get_height() / 2
    ax.text(row["Runs"] + 6, y_center,
            f'{row["Runs"]}', va="center", ha="left",
            fontsize=10, fontweight="bold", color="black")

# Segment value labels inside bars (only if wide enough)
for bar_o, bar_h, (_, row) in zip(bars_other, bars_hr, top10.iterrows()):
    if row["Other_Runs"] > 60:
        ax.text(bar_o.get_x() + bar_o.get_width() / 2,
                bar_o.get_y() + bar_o.get_height() / 2,
                f'{row["Other_Runs"]}', va="center", ha="center",
                fontsize=8, color="white", fontweight="bold")
    if row["HR_Runs"] > 40:
        ax.text(bar_h.get_x() + bar_h.get_width() / 2,
                bar_h.get_y() + bar_h.get_height() / 2,
                f'{row["HR_Runs"]}', va="center", ha="center",
                fontsize=8, color="white", fontweight="bold")

ax.set_xlabel("Runs Scored", fontsize=11)
ax.set_title("Top 10 MLB Teams by Runs Scored - 2023 Season\n(Segmented: Runs via HR vs. Other Runs)",
             fontsize=13, fontweight="bold", pad=10)
ax.set_xlim(0, 1090)
ax.spines["top"].set_visible(False)
ax.spines["right"].set_visible(False)
ax.grid(axis="x", linestyle=":", alpha=0.4)
ax.legend(loc="lower right", fontsize=9, framealpha=0.9)
fig.tight_layout()
plt.show()

Key finding: Atlanta, LA Dodgers, and Texas Rangers are separated from the rest of the field — a visible gap appears after the top three.

6 Reflection

6.1 Labeling and Annotation Design Choices

For Chart 1, I used a scatter plot to surface the positive relationship between home run production and total runs scored across all 30 MLB teams. The two annotations were chosen to anchor the extremes: the Atlanta Braves at the top-right (the best offense in baseball by nearly every measure) and the Chicago White Sox at the bottom-left (the worst). Rather than labeling every point, which would have created clutter, I used a dashed trend line to communicate the general correlation and let the two annotations do the interpretive work. Arrow-styled callout boxes with contrasting colors (red for the Braves, gray for the White Sox) ensure the annotations read clearly without blending into the data.

For Chart 2, I used a horizontal stacked bar chart to break down each team’s run production into two components: runs estimated from home runs and all other runs. Rather than a single bar per team, the segmentation lets the reader see not just how many runs a team scored but how they scored them. Color differentiates the two segments, with the Braves highlighted in a deeper red to flag them as the clear outlier. Total run labels are placed at the right end of each bar so the reader gets the summary figure without needing to mentally add the two segments.

For the formatted table, I updated the approach to pull live 2023 team batting data via pybaseball (FanGraphs), which allowed me to include additional sabermetric columns including OPS, batting WAR, and division finish, that go beyond what a static Baseball Reference table would offer. The table still sorts by wins to complement rather than duplicate the charts’ focus on runs. Division finish replaces the raw division label to add standings context at a glance, and numeric columns are consistently formatted (AVG and OPS to three decimals, bWAR to one).
